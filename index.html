UAV Anomaly Detection (Requiem)

Modern UAV systems are prime targets for sophisticated cyber-physical attacks that can inject anomalies into sensor data to mislead navigation and compromise missions. In my research, I built autonomous drones by soldering ESCs, wiring motors, configuring power boards, and managing LiPo battery systems. I installed and tuned Cube Orange+ flight controllers with PX4 firmware and Raspberry Pi computers, integrating them with the Vicon motion capture system for precise ground-truth validation. To evaluate resilience, I developed data pipelines for flight and sensor data streaming and conducted penetration testing and fault injection. I also trained a surrogate (mock) drone model that reproduced realistic sensor and position outputs, which was used to generate spoofed coordinate streams and perturbations. Using these models, we carried out Requiem, a systematic adversarial evaluation campaign that simulated coordinated attacks under controlled, repeatable conditions, enabling rigorous validation of anomaly detection. This work culminated in embedding a neural network within PX4â€™s EKF for anomaly detection and contributing to a soon-to-be 2025 publication with University of Illinois collaborators.

Rover Anomaly Detection

Much like UAVs, ground based rovers can also be compromised to make them deviate from their intended target. Even a simple delay in the mission caused by the attacker could yield to missed targets. We simulated such attacks by adding constant offset to sensor data or controlled noise. We then trained an ML based anomaly detection model to counter and detect these forms of attacks. My job was to look into the inner workings of the rover system (running ROS) and find places to inject these perturbations in the sensor data. This involved hands of testing of the roverâ€™s behavior in an effort to find the perfect amount of perturbations to inject thus tricking the roverâ€™s PID to deviate from the precise path while also resembling the original path close enough that it won;t be possible to detect it at first glance.
