UAV Anomaly Detection (Requiem)

Modern UAV systems are prime targets for sophisticated cyber-physical attacks. I built autonomous drones from scratch — soldering ESCs, configuring Cube Orange+ flight controllers with PX4, and integrating Vicon motion capture for ground-truth validation. Developed data pipelines for sensor streaming, conducted penetration testing and fault injection, and trained surrogate drone models to generate spoofed coordinate streams. Culminated in embedding a neural network within PX4's EKF and contributing to a 2025 publication with University of Illinois collaborators.

Concluded

Rover Anomaly Detection

Ground-based rovers can be compromised to deviate from mission-critical paths. We simulated attacks by injecting constant offsets and controlled noise into sensor data, then trained ML-based anomaly detection models to counter them. My role involved analyzing the rover's ROS internals to identify optimal perturbation injection points — hands-on testing to find precisely the right perturbation magnitude to deceive the PID controller without being immediately detectable.

Active Research

Requiem — Computer Vision Extension

Expanding the Requiem anomaly detection framework with a computer vision module. Calibrated a Raspberry Pi camera with an RPi board to ensure undistorted imaging and reliable visual baselines — extending Requiem's adversarial evaluation beyond sensors into the visual domain.

The next phase will test how environmental factors — background complexity, lighting, occlusion, distance, observation angle, and subject motion — affect optical flow, feature tracking, and detection under adversarial conditions.

Submitted USENIX Sec '26 2025

REQUIEM for a Drone: A Machine Learning Based Framework for Stealthy Attacks against Unmanned Autonomous Vehicles

[Lead Authors — University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign], Zaid Abu-Abbas, et al. · Co-author

Autonomous UAVs rely on sensor-based state estimation algorithms to navigate and self-correct — but these same algorithms introduce exploitable blind spots. This paper investigates how an adversary, without any knowledge of a flight controller's internal logic, can manipulate sensor inputs to cause a UAV to silently deviate from its intended mission while all onboard monitors continue to report normal operation. The work surfaces a fundamental tension in safety-critical autonomous systems: the properties that make state estimators reliable and predictable are the same properties that make them learnable — and therefore attackable. Validated through both high-fidelity simulation and real-world physical drone experiments.

Submitted HCII '26 2026

Explainable AI for NBA Win Prediction: A Phase-Aware Framework for Pre-Game and Live Outcome Forecasting

Abdulla Ali ✦, Masih Faryadi ✦, Yoseyas Surafel ✦, Zaid Abu-Abbas ✦ · These authors contributed equally

Most sports prediction systems treat pre-game forecasting and live in-game updating as separate problems — leaving fans and analysts without a coherent picture of how a game's outcome probability evolves in real time. This paper presents a unified, human-centered framework for NBA win prediction that bridges both phases within a single interpretable pipeline. Grounded in a PRISMA-guided systematic review, the framework pairs a pre-game prediction model with a live, play-by-play updating component, generating natural-language rationales grounded in basketball-relevant factors so users understand why a prediction changes as a game unfolds.

Explainable AI for NBA Predictions — Extended: Live Play-by-Play Win Probability Modeling with Phase-Aware Evaluation

Abdulla Ali ✦, Masih Faryadi ✦, Yoseyas Surafel ✦, Zaid Abu-Abbas ✦ · These authors contributed equally

This paper extends our earlier HCII framework with a deeper focus on live, in-game win probability modeling. The core contribution is a full play-by-play prediction pipeline — trained on two NBA seasons (2024–25 and 2025–26) — that continuously updates win probabilities using game-state snapshots capturing score differential, time remaining, momentum indicators, and real-time efficiency metrics. The work also addresses a largely open methodological challenge: how to rigorously evaluate live win-probability systems across different game phases in a way that is both meaningful and reproducible. The explainability component is expanded, exploring how feature attribution evolves throughout a game as contextual momentum shifts from pre-game priors toward live play-by-play dynamics.
